# Tone and Voice Guide

Detailed guidance for preserving the user's authentic communication style across all correspondence.

## Core Voice Characteristics

### Direct
- **Get to the point in the first sentence.** No preamble.
- **Lead with the most important information.** Don't bury the lede.
- **Avoid hedging language.** Use "This won't work" instead of "I'm not sure this approach might be optimal."

**Examples**:
- ❌ "I wanted to reach out regarding the proposal we discussed..."
- ✅ "The proposal has three blockers we need to address."

### Warm but Not Effusive
- **Brief acknowledgment before substance.** "Thanks for raising this" → then get to business.
- **No over-the-top pleasantries.** Avoid "I hope this email finds you well" or excessive enthusiasm.
- **Genuine appreciation.** When thanking, be specific about what and why.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "I hope this email finds you well! I'm so excited to connect with you about this wonderful opportunity!"
- ✅ "Thanks for the context. Here's what I'm thinking..."

### Concise
- **Every sentence earns its place.** Cut filler words and redundant phrases.
- **Short paragraphs.** 2-4 sentences max. Break up walls of text.
- **Active voice.** "We decided" not "The decision was made."

**Examples**:
- ❌ "In order to be able to move forward with this initiative, we will need to ensure that all stakeholders are aligned on the approach that we're planning to take."
- ✅ "Next step: align stakeholders on the approach."

### Authentic
- **Preserve the user's natural voice.** Don't sand off edges or add diplomatic padding.
- **Match the user's directness level.** If they're blunt in person, be blunt in writing.
- **No corporate jargon.** Avoid "synergy," "circle back," "leverage," "action items" unless user specifically uses them.

## Channel-Appropriate Tone

### Email
- **Slightly more formal than Slack.** Still conversational, but structured.
- **Greetings and sign-offs.** Context-dependent (see channel-specifics.md).
- **Paragraphs acceptable.** Can be longer than Slack, but still concise.

### Slack
- **More casual.** Conversational, like talking to a colleague in person.
- **Shorter bursts.** 1-2 sentence paragraphs. Use line breaks liberally.
- **Emojis sparingly.** Only if it adds clarity or lightens tone appropriately.
- **No greeting/sign-off.** Jump right in.

### MBR / Reports
- **Tightest writing.** Every word counts.
- **Executive summary style.** Lead with conclusions, then supporting data.
- **No pleasantries.** Pure substance.
- **Bullet points preferred.** Dense paragraphs discouraged.

## Audience-Aware Adjustments

### Leadership (Directors, VPs, Executives)
- **Crisper language.** Remove even more filler.
- **Bottom line first.** Decision needed? State it upfront.
- **Data-driven.** Back assertions with evidence.
- **No hand-holding.** Assume context and intelligence.

**Example**:
- ❌ "I wanted to update you on the project. We've been making good progress but ran into a few challenges..."
- ✅ "Project delayed 2 weeks due to API dependencies. Mitigation plan attached."

### Peers (Same-level colleagues)
- **More conversational.** Can include context and reasoning.
- **Collaborative tone.** "What do you think?" is appropriate.
- **Slightly longer acceptable.** But still concise.

**Example**:
- ✅ "Ran into an issue with the API endpoints—looks like they're rate-limiting us harder than expected. I'm thinking we either batch the requests or cache more aggressively. Thoughts?"

### External Contacts (Customers, Partners)
- **Slightly more formal.** But still warm and direct.
- **Set context.** Don't assume they have internal knowledge.
- **Professional but approachable.** Avoid corporate speak.

**Example**:
- ✅ "Thanks for the question about our deployment timeline. We're targeting Q2 for GA, with beta access starting in April. I'll send the detailed roadmap separately."

## What to Avoid

### Corporate Template Voice
- ❌ "Per my previous email..."
- ❌ "As per our discussion..."
- ❌ "Please don't hesitate to reach out..."
- ❌ "I hope this finds you well..."
- ❌ "Moving forward, we will endeavor to..."

### Over-Diplomatic Padding
- ❌ "I might suggest that we could potentially consider..."
- ✅ "Let's try X instead."

- ❌ "It has come to my attention that there may be some concerns about..."
- ✅ "The team raised concerns about X."

### Unnecessary Hedging
- ❌ "I think this probably won't work very well..."
- ✅ "This won't work because..."

- ❌ "It seems like we might want to consider..."
- ✅ "We should do X."

### False Enthusiasm
- ❌ "I'm super excited to share this amazing update!"
- ✅ "Quick update: X shipped yesterday."

## Examples by Scenario

### Declining a Request
- ❌ "I'm so sorry, but I'm afraid I won't be able to help with this at the moment due to bandwidth constraints..."
- ✅ "Can't take this on right now—focused on X through end of month. Could Sarah help instead?"

### Asking for Help
- ❌ "I was wondering if you might possibly have some time to help me understand..."
- ✅ "Quick question about the API behavior—do you have 10 minutes today?"

### Disagreeing
- ❌ "I understand your perspective, and while I respect your viewpoint, I'm not entirely certain that approach aligns with..."
- ✅ "I think we should go with Y instead. Here's why..."

### Giving Feedback
- ❌ "Overall this is really great work, and I just have a few tiny suggestions that might potentially improve..."
- ✅ "This is solid. Three changes: [1] X, [2] Y, [3] Z."

## Voice Preservation Checklist

Before finalizing any draft:
- [ ] First sentence gets to the point
- [ ] No throat-clearing or preamble
- [ ] Every sentence adds value
- [ ] Paragraphs are 2-4 sentences max
- [ ] Active voice throughout
- [ ] No corporate jargon unless user uses it
- [ ] Tone matches channel (email vs Slack vs report)
- [ ] Audience-appropriate (leadership vs peer vs external)
- [ ] Warm but not effusive
- [ ] Direct but not rude
- [ ] User would say this themselves
